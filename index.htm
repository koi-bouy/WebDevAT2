Introduction

Mining in Australia is a long-established and defining industry, stretching from the early gold rushes of the 1800s to the modern extraction of iron ore, coal, lithium and other minerals that support the global economy. Australia is one of the world's leading producers and exporters of minerals and fossil fuels, with operations across every state and territory and a particularly strong presence in Western Australia, Queensland and New South Wales. Economic importance of mining

The mining sector is a cornerstone of the Australian economy. It accounts for more than 10 per cent of national GDP and generates a significant share of export revenue —minerals and energy resources together represent the bulk of Australia's merchandise exports, valued in the hundreds of billions of dollars annually. Mining also contributes substantially to government revenue through company taxes and royalties, supports hundreds of thousands of jobs directly plus many more indirectly across services and supply chains, and underpins investment in regional communities and infrastructure. State and federal governments rely on mining-derived revenues to fund services such as health, education and public works, and the sector's high-wage employment boosts living standards in regional areas.

Environmental impact

Despite its economic importance, mining has profound environmental consequences. Land disturbance from open-cut and underground operations alters landscapes and habitats, leading to biosectionersity loss and soil erosion. Water resources can be contaminated by acid mine drainage and heavy metal runoff, while particulate and greenhouse gas emissions from extraction and processing contribute to air pollution and climate change. Mining has also left lasting legacies of abandoned sites and ecological disruption that remain challenging to rehabilitate. In addition, many mines are located on or near Indigenous lands, raising concerns about cultural heritage impacts and social disruption.
